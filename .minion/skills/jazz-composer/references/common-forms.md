# Common Jazz Song Forms

## 32-Bar Forms
### AABA Form (Most Common)
- **A section**: 8 bars, establishes main theme
- **A section**: 8 bars, repeat or slight variation  
- **B section**: 8 bars, contrasting bridge (often modulates)
- **A section**: 8 bars, return to main theme
- **Total**: 32 bars
- **Examples**: "I Got Rhythm", "Take the A Train"

### ABAC Form  
- **A**: 8 bars
- **B**: 8 bars (contrasting)
- **A**: 8 bars (return)
- **C**: 8 bars (new material, often climactic)
- **Total**: 32 bars

## 12-Bar Blues Form
### Basic Structure
- **Bars 1-4**: I chord (tonic)
- **Bars 5-6**: IV chord (subdominant)  
- **Bars 7-8**: I chord (tonic)
- **Bars 9-10**: V chord (dominant)
- **Bars 11-12**: I chord (tonic turnaround)

### Jazz Blues Variations
- Add ii-V progressions before changes
- Use tritone substitutions
- Incorporate diminished passing chords
- Extend with turnarounds

## 16-Bar Forms
### Common in Early Jazz
- Often AA'BB' structure
- Used in many standards like "Exactly Like You"

## Modal Forms
### Characteristics
- Static harmony for extended periods
- Focus on scale/mode rather than chord changes
- Sections defined by modal centers
- Common in 1960s jazz (Miles Davis, John Coltrane)

## Through-Composed
### Modern Approach
- No repeating sections
- Continuous development
- Common in contemporary jazz composition

## Form Markings
- **Intro**: Introduction (variable length)
- **Head**: Main melody/theme
- **Solos**: Improvised sections over form
- **Backgrounds**: Composed lines under solos
- **Shout Chorus**: Climactic ensemble section
- **Outro/Coda**: Ending section
